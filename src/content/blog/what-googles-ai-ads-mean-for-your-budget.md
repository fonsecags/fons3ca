Every time Google announces a new ad format, there are two conversations. The one in the press release, which is about innovation and the future of search. And the one that matters, which is about whether this is going to cost you more money for the same results.

The new Gemini-powered ad formats are genuinely interesting. But interesting and useful are not the same thing.

## What Google is actually doing

The new formats use Gemini to generate ad content dynamically based on what the user is searching for, what your landing page says, and what creative assets you have provided. The idea is that the ad becomes more relevant in real time rather than serving a static combination of headlines and descriptions.

In principle, that is a good thing. More relevant ads should mean better click-through rates, lower CPCs, and higher conversion rates. In practice, the results depend entirely on what you feed the system.

## What I have seen already

I have been running accounts with AI-generated creative variations for long enough to have an opinion. The headline findings:

The best-performing AI-generated copy tends to be boring. It is functional, direct, and clear. It rarely has the edge that a good human writer brings. What it does have is scale. It can test a hundred variations in the time it takes a team to approve three.

The worst-performing AI-generated copy sounds like AI. Vague superlatives, generic benefit statements, the kind of language that could describe any product in any category. When this happens, it is almost always because the inputs were weak. Poor product descriptions, a landing page that does not say much, asset groups with no real differentiation.

## The budget question

New ad formats always come with a learning period. Google's systems need data to understand which combinations work, and during that period you are essentially paying for the education.

My advice is to treat the first 4 to 6 weeks of any new format as an investment in learning, not a direct ROAS play. Set a budget you are comfortable spending on data collection. Watch the signals. Adjust after you have enough to work with.

The teams that struggle most with new formats are the ones that either expect immediate results or give up too early. Both mistakes cost more than the learning period would have.

## The part the announcement does not mention

Google's announcements focus on what the technology can do. They do not focus on what you need to do to make it work.

The new AI formats are more powerful than what came before. They are also more sensitive to the quality of your inputs. A good creative brief, clean conversion data, and clear audience signals will produce very different results than a hastily assembled asset group.

The technology has moved forward. The discipline required to use it well has not changed at all.
