Anthropic published a breakdown of the internal AI workflows they have built for their own teams. Hundreds of them, covering everything from code review to deployment to running diagnostics. The piece is aimed at developers, but buried inside it is an insight that I think matters more for marketing teams than for engineers.

The insight is this: AI performs well when it has specific, documented knowledge to act on. Generic instructions produce generic output. The teams getting the most out of AI are the ones who have taken the time to write down what they know, in enough detail that the AI can actually use it.

That sounds obvious. In practice, almost no marketing teams do it.

## What documentation actually means here

It is not about writing longer prompts. It is about capturing the things that are specific to how your business works, the things that would take a new hire six months to learn, and making them available to the AI system you are using.

The Anthropic piece calls these things "gotchas sections." The common failure points. The things that look like they should work one way but actually work another. The audience definition that performs well in the UK but falls apart in Germany. The campaign structure that makes sense on paper but causes attribution problems in practice. The bid strategy that works in Q3 but needs to be adjusted going into November.

None of this is in any playbook. It lives in the heads of the people who have been doing the work for long enough to have made the mistakes. And when AI runs workflows without access to that knowledge, it produces output that is technically correct but contextually wrong.

## The institutional knowledge problem

This is not a new problem. It predates AI. Most agencies and in-house teams have always had a version of it: knowledge concentrated in a few people, transferred informally, and lost whenever those people move on.

AI makes the problem more visible because the gap between what the system knows and what an experienced practitioner knows becomes very obvious very fast. You can see it in the output. A generated ad brief that misses the nuance of the brand. A keyword list that is correct in category but wrong for the specific audience. A reporting summary that is accurate but draws the wrong conclusion because it does not know that this particular client does not count branded search in their ROAS target.

The solution is the same as it has always been: write it down. But AI gives you a stronger incentive to actually do it, because the payoff is now immediate and concrete.

## What I am doing about it

I have started treating documentation as part of the workflow rather than something that happens after the work is done. When I make a decision that is specific to a client or a market or a campaign structure, I note it somewhere that can be referenced later.

This is slower in the short term. In the medium term it compounds. The AI-assisted work gets better because the context it has access to gets richer. New people on the team get up to speed faster. Decisions that were previously locked in someone's memory become transferable.

The teams that will do best with AI over the next few years are not the ones with the most sophisticated tools. They are the ones who take seriously the work of capturing what they know. The technology is becoming a commodity. The knowledge is not.
