Every few months someone publishes a piece declaring that AI has made Google Ads obsolete. The algorithm manages bids. Smart campaigns write the ads. Performance Max does everything automatically. Why do you need a strategist anymore?

Here's what those takes miss: the hard part was never the execution. It was always the thinking.

## The automation trap

Google Ads automation is genuinely impressive at what it does. tROAS bidding works. Responsive search ads rotate competently. PMax finds conversions you'd miss manually. I use all of it.

But automation optimises for the objective you give it, not the objective you *meant* to give it. And most advertisers don't know the difference until the results come in wrong.

The classic example: target ROAS set to 4x. Campaigns hit 4x. Revenue flatlines. Why? Because the algorithm learned to prioritise easy, low-AOV conversions from brand terms (technically hitting the target, practically useless).

That's not an automation failure. That's a strategy failure upstream of the automation.

## What AI tools actually do

LLMs are getting good at PPC operations: pulling search term reports, writing ad variations, auditing account structure. I use Claude regularly for this. It's genuinely useful.

What they're not good at is the conversation you need to have before you touch the account. The one where you figure out that the client's real problem isn't conversion rate. It's that they're targeting the wrong customer segment entirely.

That conversation requires understanding the business, the market, and what success actually looks like for this specific company. AI can help you execute once you know that. It can't tell you what to know.

## The strategist's job now

Performance marketing is splitting into two tracks. Track one: the mechanics (bidding, feed management, attribution, reporting). AI is eating this fast and I'm not fighting it. Track two: diagnosis, strategy, and the ability to tell a client something they don't want to hear.

Track two is getting *more* valuable as track one gets automated. The people who survive in this industry are the ones who can hold that difference clearly.

The job hasn't disappeared. It's just moved upstream.
