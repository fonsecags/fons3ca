Meta recently published a piece on how they scale the building and testing of their most advanced AI models. It is written for researchers and engineers. But if you read it as someone who runs campaigns on Meta's platforms every day, there are things in there that change how you think about what you are doing.

The core of it is this: at the scale Meta operates, the way you test an AI system matters as much as the system itself. Getting the evaluation right, catching failures early, and being able to iterate quickly on a model that serves billions of interactions is as hard as building the model.

That problem is structurally identical to the one performance marketers face at a smaller scale.

## Testing is the work

In Meta's model development, the teams that move fastest are not the ones with the biggest models. They are the ones with the most rigorous testing infrastructure. They know quickly when something is working, know quickly when it is not, and can iterate without breaking what was already working.

Most ad accounts are run the opposite way. Creative is produced and launched. Results come back weeks later. Changes are made based on incomplete data. The feedback loop is too slow to learn anything reliable.

I run accounts with weekly creative tests and a defined methodology for what constitutes a result worth acting on. The accounts that do this consistently outperform the ones that do not, not because the creative is always better, but because the learning is faster.

Meta has built billion-dollar infrastructure to move fast and test rigorously. The lesson for advertisers is not complicated: do the same, at the scale you can afford.

## What rigorous testing actually looks like in an ad account

It starts with knowing what you are testing before you test it. A hypothesis. Not "let us try a video" but "we believe video outperforms static for this audience segment at awareness stage because the buying consideration is high and people need more information to engage."

Then you isolate the variable. One change at a time. If you change the creative and the audience and the landing page simultaneously and results improve, you have learned nothing about why.

Then you define success in advance. What result, over what time period, with what confidence, would cause you to adopt this approach? If you do not define this before you start, you will read results selectively after.

Most teams skip all three steps. They test chaotically, change too many things at once, and interpret results based on what they hoped to see. Meta's engineers do not do this. You should not either.

## The compounding effect

Meta's piece on AI development emphasises something I think applies directly to advertising: rigorous testing compounds over time. Each cycle of learning feeds the next. The system gets smarter not because any single insight was brilliant but because the process of generating and acting on insights is reliable.

Ad accounts work the same way. An account with a strong testing culture that has been running for two years will outperform a freshly structured account with a bigger budget in almost every case I have seen. The accumulated learning is worth more than the spend.

Build the process. Run the tests. Document what you learn. The compounding is real, and it is the most underrated advantage in performance marketing.
