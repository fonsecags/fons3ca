When I was running digital strategy for Renault, one of the things that struck me early was how badly most automotive campaigns misunderstood local intent. People were not searching for "car dealer near me" the way we assumed. They were searching for specific models, specific services, specific problems they needed solving. The intent was local, but the signal was specific.

That gap between assumed intent and actual intent is still where most local campaigns fail. And it has gotten wider.

## How local search has changed

A few years ago, local search was relatively simple. Someone needed a service. They searched for it with a location. They clicked on the nearest result with decent reviews.

That model still exists, but it describes a smaller portion of local search behaviour than it used to. What has grown is a more complex pattern. Research online, often across multiple sessions and devices, followed by a decision to act locally. The intent is local from the beginning, but it does not look local in the search data until the final stages.

Google's expansion of Local Services Ads reflects this shift. The format is designed to capture people who are further along in a decision, not people who are just starting to look. The underlying assumption is that the consideration phase happened somewhere else, and the LSA is there to convert someone who is already close to acting.

That assumption is mostly right. And it has implications for how you structure campaigns around it.

## What this means in practice

If you are running local campaigns that treat awareness and conversion as the same moment, you are probably losing people in the middle. They saw you at the top of the funnel, they went away to think about it, they came back with higher intent, and your campaign either did not reach them in that second moment or reached them with the wrong message.

The fix is not complicated but it requires accepting that local campaigns need more structure than most teams give them. Upper funnel activity that builds awareness and captures data. Retargeting that stays present through the consideration phase. And LSA or high-intent search campaigns that are there when the decision is being made.

Most local campaigns skip the middle. They go from awareness to conversion with nothing in between, and then wonder why conversion rates are low despite good awareness numbers.

## The role of data quality

Local campaign performance is more sensitive to data quality than most other campaign types. The signals that drive local decisions, reviews, location data, service history, are often incomplete or inconsistent in the way they feed into campaign systems.

I spend a significant amount of time on local campaigns just cleaning up the inputs. Making sure business profiles are complete and accurate. Making sure conversion tracking is capturing real actions, not just clicks. Making sure the audience data reflects actual local customers, not broad geographic pools.

The campaigns are only as good as what goes into them. In local, the inputs degrade faster than in other categories because they depend on real-world information that changes constantly.

## The bigger point

Local intent is more sophisticated than it was five years ago. Customers move through longer journeys with more touchpoints before they act. The campaigns built for a simpler version of that behaviour are leaving results on the table.

The solution is not more spend. It is better structure, better data, and a clearer understanding of where in the journey your customer actually is when they find you.
